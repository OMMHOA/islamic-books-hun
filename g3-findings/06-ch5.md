## [FIX-BOTH] AR p.136 / HUN ~line 2295 / ENG ~line 2336
AR: «كان المربد قبل أن يتخذ مصلى كهذه المصليات التى تنتشر فى **ريفنا**» — "before it was made into a prayer-ground like these musallayat that abound in **our** countryside" (al-Ghazali is Egyptian; "our countryside" = his own, i.e. Egypt).
ENG: "...like the mosques which abound in the **Saudi Arabian** countryside."
HUN: "...mielőtt olyan mecsetté fejlesztették, amilyenek a **szaúd-arábiai** vidéken bőven találhatók..."
→ The Arabic says "our countryside" (ريفنا), not Saudi Arabia — the English edition inserted a country name that isn't in the original (and picked the wrong one; al-Ghazali would mean Egypt), and HUN copied the fabricated detail. Recommend replacing with something country-neutral matching the Arabic, e.g. "amilyenek nálunk / a mi vidékünkön bőven találhatók."

## [NOTE] AR p.155 / HUN ~line 2651 / ENG ~line 2685 (physical description hadith of Hind ibn Abī Hālah)
AR: «...جلُّ نظره الملاحظة... **يسوق أصحابه** ويبدأ من لقيه بالسلام»
ENG: "...he did not stare. **He led his Companions** and he first greeted anyone he meets with a salām."
HUN: "...nem bámult. **Társai élén járt**, és elsőként köszöntött szalāmmal mindenkit, akivel találkozott."
→ «يسوق» (sāqa) literally means "to drive/herd [from behind]," and in classical commentary on this specific Shamā'il ḥadīth is often understood as the Prophet (ﷺ) walking *behind* his Companions, ushering them ahead out of humility — the opposite of "leading [from the front]." ENG/HUN render it as "led," which may reverse the intended nuance. Flagging as NOTE (not certain enough for FIX-BOTH without checking an authoritative Shamā'il al-Tirmidhī translation) — worth a second look.

## [NOTE] AR p.155 / HUN ~line 2657 / ENG ~line 2691
AR: «...**جلَّ ضحكه التبسُّم**...» — "...most of his laughter [amounted to no more than] a smile [i.e. he rarely laughed outright]..."
ENG: "...**His laugh was less than a smile**..."
HUN: "...**Nevetése kevesebb volt a mosolynál**..."
→ Awkward, near-unparseable phrasing inherited from the English ("a laugh being 'less than' a smile" doesn't quite make sense as a comparison) — the intended meaning is that he rarely laughed aloud, mostly just smiled. Not flagging as a factual error since the gist is recoverable from context, but the Hungarian reads as an odd calque; a clearer rendering would be something like "Nevetése ritkán haladta meg a mosolyt" / "leginkább csak mosolygott, hangosan ritkán nevetett."
